Module 1: Introduction to Prompt Engineering

What Is Prompt Engineering?

Prompt engineering is the discipline of designing, structuring, and refining inputs to AI language models to produce accurate, relevant, and useful outputs. It bridges the gap between human intent and machine understanding.

Key Insight: The quality of your AI output is directly proportional to the quality of your input. A well-crafted prompt can transform a generic AI response into an expert-level deliverable.

How LLMs Process Your Prompts

  1. Tokenization — Your text is broken into tokens (words, subwords, or characters)
  2. Attention — The model weighs which tokens are most relevant to each other
  3. Pattern Matching — The model identifies patterns from its training data
  4. Generation — Tokens are generated one at a time, each influenced by all prior tokens

Key LLM Parameters

ParameterWhat It ControlsTypical Range
TemperatureCreativity vs. consistency of output0.0 (deterministic) – 2.0 (very creative)
Max TokensMaximum length of the response100 – 4,096+
Top-pNucleus sampling threshold for diversity0.1 – 1.0
System MessagePersistent instructions that frame all responsesN/A (text)

The Prompt Engineering Spectrum

Stage 1 — Ad Hoc: "Write me something about AI"
Stage 2 — Structured: "Write a 500-word article about AI in healthcare for a business audience"
Stage 3 — Professional: Full CRAFT prompt with context, role, action, format, and tone specifications

Module 2: The CRAFT Framework Deep Dive

The CRAFT Framework

CRAFT is a structured methodology for composing effective prompts. Each letter represents an essential component:

C
Context — Background information the AI needs. Include domain, situation, constraints, and any relevant data. The more specific context you provide, the more relevant the output.
R
Role — The expert persona the AI should adopt. Specify domain expertise, years of experience, communication style, and perspective. Example: "Act as a senior data scientist with 10 years of experience in healthcare analytics."
A
Action — The specific task to perform. Use precise action verbs: analyze, compare, create, evaluate, summarize, design, recommend, critique, transform, explain.
F
Format — How the output should be structured. Specify: bullet points, numbered list, table, JSON, markdown, specific headings, word count, or exact template to follow.
T
Tone — The voice and style of the response. Consider: formal/casual, technical/accessible, direct/diplomatic, encouraging/critical, concise/detailed.

CRAFT in Action: Example

Before (Vague)
Write a report about our sales.
After (CRAFT)
[Context] Our SaaS company had Q3 revenue of $2.4M (up 15% QoQ). Enterprise deals grew 40% but SMB churn increased to 8%. We're preparing for the board meeting next Tuesday. [Role] Act as a senior business analyst who specializes in SaaS metrics and board communications. [Action] Create a Q3 sales performance report that highlights wins, identifies the SMB churn risk, and recommends 3 actionable steps for Q4. [Format] Structure as: Executive Summary (3 sentences), Key Metrics table, Wins section (bullets), Risk Analysis, Q4 Recommendations (numbered). [Tone] Professional and data-driven, suitable for board-level presentation. Be direct about risks while maintaining confidence in the growth trajectory.

Action Verb Categories

CategoryVerbsUse When
AnalysisAnalyze, evaluate, compare, assess, diagnoseBreaking down complex information
CreationWrite, draft, compose, design, developGenerating new content
TransformationSummarize, translate, rewrite, simplify, expandChanging existing content
DecisionRecommend, prioritize, rank, select, justifyMaking or supporting decisions

Few-Shot Learning

Tip: Three examples is the sweet spot. Fewer may not establish the pattern; more can waste context tokens without improvement.
Example: Sentiment Classification
Classify the sentiment of customer reviews as POSITIVE, NEGATIVE, or NEUTRAL. Examples: Review: "The product exceeded my expectations!" → POSITIVE Review: "Shipping was delayed by 2 weeks." → NEGATIVE Review: "It works as described, nothing special." → NEUTRAL Now classify: Review: "I love the new interface but the load time is frustrating."

Chain-of-Thought Prompting

Without CoT
A store has 45 shirts. They sell 60% on Monday and half the remainder on Tuesday. How many are left?

AI may jump to an answer, possibly incorrectly.

With CoT
A store has 45 shirts. They sell 60% on Monday and half the remainder on Tuesday. How many are left? Think through this step by step, showing your work at each stage.

AI shows: 45 × 0.6 = 27 sold Mon, 18 remain, 18 / 2 = 9 left.

Multi-Persona Engineering

Panel Discussion Technique: Ask the AI to analyze a decision from multiple expert perspectives. Example: "Evaluate this product launch plan from the perspective of the CEO (growth focus), CFO (financial risk), and CTO (technical feasibility). Have each expert provide their top 3 concerns."

Module 4: Context Engineering

What Is Context Engineering?

Context engineering goes beyond prompt writing. It is the discipline of designing and managing the information environment in which an AI model operates. While a prompt is a single instruction, context engineering manages the AI's entire working memory.

The 4 Layers of Context

LayerContentToken Budget
System InstructionsPersistent rules, persona, constraints5-10%
Reference MaterialDocuments, data, examples the AI needs40-60%
Conversation HistoryPrior messages providing continuity20-30%
Current TaskThe immediate instruction/question10-20%

Context Budgeting

Warning: More context is not always better. Overloading the context window dilutes attention and can actually reduce output quality. Be selective about what you include.

Information Priority Stack

  1. Essential — Without this, the AI cannot complete the task (core data, key constraints)
  2. Important — Significantly improves quality (domain terminology, style examples)
  3. Helpful — Adds nuance and polish (edge cases, preferences)
  4. Nice-to-have — Marginal improvement (background history, related context)
Rule of Thumb: If removing a piece of context doesn't change the output, it probably doesn't need to be there. Prioritize ruthlessly.

Module 5: Prompt Chains & Workflows

What Are Prompt Chains?

Prompt chains break complex tasks into a series of simpler prompts where the output of one becomes the input to the next. This mirrors how experts decompose problems: research first, then outline, then draft, then refine.

Three Workflow Patterns

PatternDescriptionExample
SequentialSteps run in order, each using the previous outputResearch → Outline → Draft → Edit
Fan-OutSame prompt applied to multiple inputs in parallelAnalyze 10 customer reviews simultaneously
IterativeSame prompt applied repeatedly, refining each timeDraft → Critique → Revise → Critique → Final

Task Decomposition

Monolithic Prompt
Research AI trends, create an outline, write a 2000-word article, add citations, and format it for our blog with SEO optimization.

Too many tasks. Output quality suffers on each.

Decomposed Chain
Step 1: Research the top 5 AI trends for 2025 with sources. Step 2: Create a blog outline from these trends for a business audience. Step 3: Write the article following this outline (2000 words). Step 4: Add SEO metadata and format for WordPress.

Each step gets full attention. Quality compounds.

Case Study: Content Production Workflow

Step 1: Research
Extract the 5 most important statistics about remote work adoption in 2024-2025. For each, provide the source, the exact figure, and a one-sentence interpretation.
Step 2: Outline
Using these statistics, create a blog outline with: attention-grabbing title, introduction hook, 5 sections (one per stat), and a conclusion with call to action. Target audience: HR directors.
Step 3: Draft
Write the full blog post following this outline. 1500 words. Professional but engaging tone. Include the statistics naturally in the narrative. End each section with a practical takeaway.
Step 4: Polish
Review this blog post. Fix any awkward transitions, strengthen the opening paragraph, ensure all statistics have proper attribution, and add 3 suggested social media snippets for promotion.

Module 6: Testing & Refining Prompts

The Prompt Engineering Cycle

Define Criteria → Write Prompt → Test → Evaluate → Diagnose → Refine → Repeat

Key Principle: Professional prompt engineering is iterative. Expect to refine a prompt 3-5 times before it consistently produces the quality you need.

Common Failure Modes & Fixes

Failure ModeSymptomsFix
HallucinationAI invents facts, cites non-existent sourcesAdd "Only use information provided" constraint; request source citations; use retrieval-augmented generation
VerbosityResponse is 3x longer than neededSpecify word/sentence limits; add "Be concise"; use format constraints (bullet points, tables)
Off-TopicResponse drifts from the original requestStrengthen the Action element; add "Focus exclusively on X"; remove distracting context
InconsistencySame prompt gives different quality resultsLower temperature; add few-shot examples; use more specific instructions; add output validation
Shallow AnalysisSurface-level response lacking depthAdd Chain-of-Thought; request "detailed analysis with evidence"; specify depth expectations

Validation Techniques

  • Self-Check: Ask the AI to critique its own output against your criteria
  • Rubric Scoring: Define 5 quality dimensions, score each 1-5
  • Red Teaming: Try to break the prompt with edge cases and adversarial inputs
  • A/B Testing: Run two prompt versions on the same inputs, compare results

Response Prediction

Tip: Before running a prompt, predict what the output will look like. If you can't predict it, the prompt probably isn't specific enough. This is the fastest diagnostic tool in your toolkit.

Building a Personal Prompt Library

Why: Your best prompts are an asset. Organizing, versioning, and sharing them turns individual expertise into team capability.

How to organize:

  • Group by task type (writing, analysis, coding, communication)
  • Track versions — keep iteration history so you know what improved
  • Note the model — prompts may behave differently across models
  • Tag with metadata: use case, domain, confidence level, last tested date
  • Share with your team — prompt libraries multiply in value with collaboration

CRAFT Framework Checklist

  • Context — Have I provided sufficient background information?
  • Role — Have I defined the expert persona?
  • Action — Is my instruction specific and unambiguous?
  • Format — Have I specified the output structure?

Prompt Quality Self-Check

  • Can I predict what the output will look like? (If not, prompt needs work)
  • Could someone else use this prompt and get the same quality result?
  • Have I removed unnecessary information that could distract the model?
  • Am I asking for one clear task, not bundling multiple tasks?
  • Have I tested with at least 2-3 different inputs?
